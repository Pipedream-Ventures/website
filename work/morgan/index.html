01 — The Concept

The living room you wish you had — where the shelves happen to be shoppable

Morning: specialty coffee, pastries, a warm place to sit and work. Evening: craft cocktails, natural wine, small plates. Always: a curated vintage clothing and home goods section personally sourced by the founders.

Not three businesses duct-taped together — one cohesive space with a personality that shifts from day to night. The model is proven and trending nationally. Cuties (Portland, ME), Agency (Milwaukee), and Two Dollar Radio HQ (Columbus) all operate as day-to-night hybrids.

02 — Target Customer

Who's walking through the door

Primary: Women 25-45 in Columbus who care about aesthetics, experience, and community. They follow vintage accounts on Instagram, shop at Grandview Mercantile, brunch in German Village, and want a "third place" that isn't a chain.

Secondary: Remote workers who want a beautiful place to post up during the day. Couples looking for an evening spot that isn't another brewery. Weekend visitors exploring Columbus neighborhoods.

The gap

Nobody in Columbus does all three well — good coffee, good cocktails, and something to browse — in one curated space with real taste behind it. Fox in the Snow does pastries. Law Bird does cocktails. Grandview Mercantile does vintage. Nobody combines them.

05b — The Lean Path

How to do it without a big loan

The $275K number assumes building from scratch. You don't have to. Here's how to cut the startup cost by 60-70% and skip the SBA entirely.

The three biggest cost-killers

1. Take over an already-built-out space

That 970 Parsons Ave listing? It's a fully renovated bar — kitchen, restrooms, patio, done. If the previous tenant was a bar or coffee shop, you inherit their build-out. The $90-180K build-out line drops to $15-30K in cosmetic changes to make it yours.

2. Skip the D-5 — start beer + wine only

D-1 (beer) + D-2 (wine) are not quota-limited the same way as the D-5. Cheaper, faster, no $20-50K transfer fee. Serve natural wine, craft beer, and batched cocktails. Add full spirits in year 2 when cash flow supports the D-5 purchase.

3. Finance equipment separately

Espresso machines and bar equipment qualify for equipment financing — basically lease-to-own. Often $0 down, $500-800/mo payments. That's $20-40K you don't need upfront.

The Phased Approach

Before signing any lease, prove the concept with zero risk:

  1. Pop up first. Vintage pop-ups at Columbus flea markets, farmers markets, local events ($0-200/booth). Build the Instagram. Generate actual revenue.
  2. Pop-up coffee + vintage at an existing space — rent someone's bar or restaurant on Saturday mornings when they're closed.
  3. Walk into an SBA meeting with real sales data instead of projections. Bankers love proof.
  4. Sign a lease on a built-out space when you're ready — use pop-up revenue + savings. No bank needed.
The Real Move

Start selling vintage and pouring coffee at markets this spring. Open the real space when the brand already has a following. That's how you go from "we need $275K" to "we need a weekend, a card table, and good taste."

06 — Business Structure

How to structure Morgan + Scarlet

Two co-founders need a clear operating agreement from day one. This is where partnerships either work or blow up. Get it right early.

Recommended

New LLC (50/50)

Form a new Ohio LLC specifically for this business. Equal ownership, clean operating agreement. Separate from Seth's entities (Pipedream / Rapchat). Seth can invest or loan money without being a member. Keeps liability clean for everyone.

Under Pipedream Ventures

Could house the business under Seth's existing LLC. Simpler setup but tangles Morgan's business with Seth's other ventures. If Morgan and Scarlet are equal partners, this gets complicated. Not recommended.

The Operating Agreement Must Cover
  • Ownership split — 50/50? Based on capital contribution? Sweat equity?
  • Decision-making — Who has final call on what? (e.g., Morgan on design/curation, Scarlet on operations?)
  • Capital contributions — How much does each person put in? Is Seth's money a loan or investment?
  • Salary vs. distributions — When do founders start taking a salary? What happens before the business is profitable?
  • Exit/buyout — If one person wants out in 3 years, what happens? Define it now.
  • Day-to-day roles — Who does what? Avoid "we both do everything" — that breeds resentment.

Budget $2,000-$4,000 for a business attorney to draft this. It's the best money you'll spend.

Funding the $275K

  • SBA 7(a) loan: Up to $5M, typically 10-20% down. Good for first-time owners. Huntington Bank is Columbus's strongest SBA lender.
  • Personal savings + loan combo: If you can put $60-80K down between the two of you, finance the rest.
  • Seth as lender (not owner): A formal loan from Pipedream or personal funds keeps ownership clean. Pay it back with interest. Document everything.
  • Skip outside investors. For a lifestyle business this size, 100% founder ownership is worth protecting.
07 — The Liquor License

Ohio's liquor licensing — the real story

This is the single biggest regulatory hurdle. Ohio's system is old, quota-based, and not intuitive. Here's what actually matters.

How Ohio's System Works

Ohio uses a quota system. A limited number of D-class permits are allocated to each city/township based on U.S. Census population data — roughly 1 permit per 1,000 residents. Franklin County (Columbus) is a large market, so permits do exist, but demand is high.

This means you usually cannot simply apply for a new permit. If the quota is full in your area, you need to buy and transfer an existing permit from another business.

Good News (June 2025)

Ohio launched OPAL (Ohio Permit and Licensing) — a fully digital system replacing all paper applications. This makes the process faster and more transparent. All applications, transfers, renewals, and tracking happen online at com.ohio.gov/opal.

What You Need

Permit What It Covers Annual State Fee
D-5 All alcoholic beverages (beer, wine, spirits, mixed) for on-premises consumption ~$2,344
D-6 Sunday sales (add-on) Additional
D-3A Extended hours to 2:30 AM (add-on) Additional

The D-5 is the gold standard — one permit that covers everything. The alternative is stacking D-1 (beer, $376) + D-2 (wine, $564) + D-3 (spirits, $750) + D-3A (late hours) + D-6 (Sunday). Same privileges, more paperwork.

The Real Cost: Transfer Market

State fees are cheap. The transfer cost is not. Buying a D-5 permit on the secondary market in Franklin County runs $20,000 - $50,000+ depending on availability and demand.

Two broker marketplaces actively list Franklin County D-5 permits:

  • LiquorLicense.com — largest national broker, has a dedicated Ohio/Franklin County D-5 page
  • Liquor License Auctioneers (LLA) — online marketplace, Franklin County page at liquorlicenseauctioneers.com

Plan B: Start Without Spirits

If the D-5 timeline or cost is prohibitive, open with D-1 (beer) + D-2 (wine/mixed) first. These are easier to get and cheaper. Serve craft beer, natural wine, and pre-mixed cocktails on day one. Add the D-3 (spirits) later when you find and transfer the permit. Many successful bars have launched this way.

Other Permits & Registrations

  • Ohio food service license — Columbus Public Health
  • Columbus business license — City of Columbus
  • ServSafe certification — At least one manager/owner
  • Ohio vendor's license — Sales tax collection for retail goods
  • Building/occupancy permits — Columbus Dept of Building & Zoning
  • Fire inspection — Required before opening
  • Signage permit — Columbus has district-specific rules

German Village note: The German Village Commission reviews exterior changes to buildings. Budget extra time for signage or facade approvals.

09 — Honest Assessment

Why it works — and what could go wrong

Why This Works in Columbus

Validated hybrid model
Coffee-bar hybrids are a documented trend in Columbus. Nobody is adding vintage retail to the mix.
German Village wants this
Affluent, walkable, design-conscious. Law Bird (Food & Wine Top 10). Fox in the Snow. The audience exists. The hybrid concept doesn't.
Columbus vintage scene is thriving
Grandview Mercantile does $4.9M/yr. Flower Child Vintage, Ohio Thrift, dozens of Instagram sellers prove demand.
The economics favor hybrids
Coffee-only nets $40-60K. Bar-only is evenings. Hybrid maximizes every daypart. Vintage adds high-margin revenue with minimal labor.
Two founders > one
Morgan and Scarlet can split day/night shifts, share the capital burden, and keep each other sane during year one.

What Could Go Wrong

Running out of money
~60% of coffee shops close in 5 years, mostly from undercapitalization. Budget 12 months of runway, not 6.
Liquor license delays
Ohio's quota system is slow and expensive. Mitigate: start with beer + wine only (Plan B), add spirits later.
Build-out surprises
Old GV buildings hide plumbing/electrical nightmares. Budget 20% overage and get a thorough inspection before signing.
Doing too much at once
Launch coffee first. Add bar in month 2. Refine retail over time. Don't try to perfect all three on day one.
Partnership tension
The operating agreement must be airtight. Define roles, decisions, exits, and money before you sign a lease.
