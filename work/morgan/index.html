Columbus, Ohio · What Would It Take?

Cocktails & vintage.

A cocktail bar with a curated vintage clothing and home goods section. Evening drinks, shoppable shelves, one space with real taste behind it. What would it actually take to make this happen in Columbus?

01 — The Concept

The living room you wish you had — where the shelves happen to be shoppable

Craft cocktails, natural wine, great beer. A curated vintage clothing and home goods section that makes people want to stay, browse, and buy. Not a bar with some stuff on the walls — a space where the curation IS the vibe. And on special nights: intimate dinners, private events, and collaborations in the most beautifully designed room in the neighborhood.

Both founders are corporate designers at Abercrombie & Fitch. They build spaces and visual worlds for a living. This isn't going to look like someone's first business — it's going to look like it belongs in Architectural Digest. That design sense is the product. The cocktails and vintage are what you buy. The space is why you come back and why you post about it.

Law Bird in German Village just made Food & Wine's Top 10 US Bars. Grandview Mercantile does $4.9M/year in vintage. Nobody in Columbus is combining the two with a design-forward space that also hosts. That's the gap.

Why This + Why Now

Columbus coffee-bar hybrids are a documented trend (Columbus Foodletter, Feb 2025). Natural wine bars are exploding nationally. Vintage is bigger than ever. But nobody in Columbus is doing cocktails + curated vintage as one concept. The audience exists — they're just spread across five different places right now.

The Switchyards Signal

Switchyards just opened at 368 E Whittier in German Village — a "neighborhood work club" for creatives, freelancers, and remote workers. All memberships sold out on drop day. $129/mo, waitlisted immediately. They have free coffee, quiet work areas, and a beautiful designed space. What they don't have: a bar, food, events, or anything to do after 5 PM.

A cocktail bar + vintage shop nearby (or even next door) would be the natural "after work" destination for every single Switchyards member. They work there by day, they drink at your place by night. That's a built-in customer pipeline.

Clintonville Angle: Coworking Gap

There is no real coworking space in Clintonville. COhatch is in Dublin, Worthington, Upper Arlington, Easton — not Clintonville. Haven Collective is in Bexley. Switchyards is in GV. The Clintonville creative crowd has nowhere to go.

A daytime-to-evening concept in Clintonville could fill this gap: casual workspace by day (coffee, wifi, tables), cocktail bar + vintage by evening. This is the one scenario where adding simple coffee/daytime service makes strategic sense — not as a coffee shop, but as a work club that turns into a bar. Think Switchyards + cocktails + vintage, all in one. Clintonville would lose its mind.

02 — Target Customer

Who's walking through the door

Primary: Women 25-45 in Columbus who care about aesthetics, experience, and community. They follow vintage accounts on Instagram, shop at Grandview Mercantile, brunch in German Village, and want a "third place" that isn't a chain.

Secondary: Remote workers who want a beautiful place to post up during the day. Couples looking for an evening spot that isn't another brewery. Weekend visitors exploring Columbus neighborhoods.

The gap

Law Bird does cocktails. Grandview Mercantile does vintage. Nobody combines them. The person who drinks at Law Bird and shops at Grandview Mercantile is the same person — and right now she's making two trips.

Events Are the Sleeper Revenue Stream

A beautifully designed 1,500 SF space in German Village with a cocktail bar? That's a private event venue that happens to be open to the public most nights. Think:

  • Private rentals: Birthday parties, bridal showers, corporate events, brand launches. $1,500-$4,000/night for the space + minimum bar spend. In German Village? This books itself.
  • Special dinners: Collaborate with local chefs for 20-30 seat intimate dinners. $85-150/head, BYOB-style where the bar is already there. Columbus foodies eat this up. One dinner = $2,000-$4,500 in a single night.
  • Vintage trunk shows: Partner with other vintage sellers for curated shopping nights with cocktails. Press magnet, community builder, sells inventory.
  • Brand collaborations: Local brands (candles, ceramics, jewelry) pay to pop up in your space. $200-500/night or revenue share.

Two A&F designers building the space means it photographs like a dream. Every event guest becomes a marketer. Every Instagram story is a free ad.

The Space Is the Portfolio

Two A&F corporate designers running a beautifully designed bar? That's a living showroom. Interior design and home staging is pure gravy — no extra inventory, no extra lease, no license. Just taste and time.

  • Home staging: Columbus median home price is ~$280K. Staging fees run $1,500-$5,000/home. A realtor grabs a cocktail, loves the space, asks "who designed this?" — that's a lead that walked in the door.
  • Interior design clients: Same pipeline. The bar is the portfolio. The vintage sourcing skills translate directly — clients want that curated, one-of-a-kind look.
  • Zero startup cost: They're already designing the space and sourcing vintage. This revenue stream costs nothing extra to add.
The Math

Cocktails: 75-85% gross margin. Vintage: 60-70%. Events: nearly pure profit (space is already paid for). Design/staging: 80%+ margin (it's their time). No morning staff, no 5 AM opens. Net margin for this model: 18-25%. Owner income at Year 2: $115K-$190K — and that's with one operator. Multiple revenue streams from a single space is what makes this more than just a bar.

06 — Business Structure

How to structure it

Depends on who's in. A few options depending on how roles shake out.

Simplest

Scarlet's LLC, sole owner

Scarlet forms a new Ohio LLC. She owns and operates it. Morgan helps with curation and design as a friend, not a partner. Seth/Morgan can lend money via a formal note. Clean, simple, no partnership complexity.

Morgan + Scarlet LLC

If Morgan wants to be involved formally — new 50/50 LLC with a real operating agreement. Requires defining: who decides what, who puts in what, and what happens if someone wants out. More upside, more complexity.

If there's a partnership, the operating agreement must cover
  • Ownership split — 50/50? Based on capital contribution? Sweat equity?
  • Decision-making — Who has final call on what?
  • Capital contributions — How much does each person put in?
  • Salary vs. distributions — When do founders start taking a salary?
  • Exit/buyout — If one person wants out in 3 years, what happens? Define it now.

Budget $2,000-$4,000 for a business attorney to draft this. Worth every dollar.

Funding

  • Personal savings: The lean path ($57-95K) is potentially fundable without a bank loan at all.
  • SBA 7(a) loan: 10-20% down, good for first-timers. Huntington Bank is Columbus's strongest SBA lender.
  • Equipment financing: Bar equipment can be financed separately, often $0 down.
  • Friends & family as lenders (not owners): Formal loan with interest. Documented. Keeps ownership clean.
  • Skip outside investors. For a business this size, 100% founder ownership is worth protecting.
07 — The Liquor License

Ohio's liquor licensing — the real story

This is the single biggest regulatory hurdle. Ohio's system is old, quota-based, and not intuitive. Here's what actually matters.

How Ohio's System Works

Ohio uses a quota system. A limited number of D-class permits are allocated to each city/township based on U.S. Census population data — roughly 1 permit per 1,000 residents. Franklin County (Columbus) is a large market, so permits do exist, but demand is high.

This means you usually cannot simply apply for a new permit. If the quota is full in your area, you need to buy and transfer an existing permit from another business.

Good News (June 2025)

Ohio launched OPAL (Ohio Permit and Licensing) — a fully digital system replacing all paper applications. This makes the process faster and more transparent. All applications, transfers, renewals, and tracking happen online at com.ohio.gov/opal.

What You Need

Permit What It Covers Annual State Fee
D-5 All alcoholic beverages (beer, wine, spirits, mixed) for on-premises consumption ~$2,344
D-6 Sunday sales (add-on) Additional
D-3A Extended hours to 2:30 AM (add-on) Additional

The D-5 is the gold standard — one permit that covers everything. The alternative is stacking D-1 (beer, $376) + D-2 (wine, $564) + D-3 (spirits, $750) + D-3A (late hours) + D-6 (Sunday). Same privileges, more paperwork.

The Real Cost: Transfer Market

State fees are cheap. The transfer cost is not. Buying a D-5 permit on the secondary market in Franklin County runs $20,000 - $50,000+ depending on availability and demand.

Two broker marketplaces actively list Franklin County D-5 permits:

  • LiquorLicense.com — largest national broker, has a dedicated Ohio/Franklin County D-5 page
  • Liquor License Auctioneers (LLA) — online marketplace, Franklin County page at liquorlicenseauctioneers.com

The Process

1
Find a Permit
Browse brokers or hire an attorney. Watch the market for 2-3 months.
2
Negotiate & Agree
Purchase agreement with the seller. $20K-$50K for a D-5 in Franklin County.
3
Apply via OPAL
Submit transfer application online. Include financial verification, entity docs, lease agreement.
4
Approval
60-120 days if no objections. DOLC reviews, inspects, and issues.
Plan B: Start Without Spirits

If the D-5 timeline or cost is prohibitive, open with D-1 (beer) + D-2 (wine/mixed) first. These are easier to get and cheaper. Serve craft beer, natural wine, and pre-mixed cocktails on day one. Add the D-3 (spirits) later when you find and transfer the permit. Many successful bars have launched this way.

Other Permits & Registrations

  • Ohio food service license — Columbus Public Health
  • Columbus business license — City of Columbus
  • ServSafe certification — At least one manager/owner
  • Ohio vendor's license — Sales tax collection for retail goods
  • Building/occupancy permits — Columbus Dept of Building & Zoning
  • Fire inspection — Required before opening
  • Signage permit — Columbus has district-specific rules

German Village note: The German Village Commission reviews exterior changes to buildings. Budget extra time for signage or facade approvals.

09 — Honest Assessment

Why it works — and what could go wrong

Why This Works in Columbus

Two professional designers building the space
Both founders design spaces and visual worlds at A&F for a living. This will be the most Instagrammed room in the neighborhood. Every guest is a marketer. Every event photo is a free ad.
German Village wants this
Affluent, walkable, design-conscious. Law Bird (Food & Wine Top 10). Fox in the Snow. The audience exists. The hybrid concept doesn't.
Columbus vintage scene is thriving
Grandview Mercantile does $4.9M/yr. Flower Child Vintage, Ohio Thrift, dozens of Instagram sellers prove demand.
The economics favor hybrids
Coffee-only nets $40-60K. Bar-only is evenings. Hybrid maximizes every daypart. Vintage adds high-margin revenue with minimal labor.
No morning shift
No 5 AM opens. Evenings only (plus weekend afternoons). Better quality of life in year one when the founder IS the staff.

What Could Go Wrong

Running out of money
~60% of coffee shops close in 5 years, mostly from undercapitalization. Budget 12 months of runway, not 6.
Liquor license delays
Ohio's quota system is slow and expensive. Mitigate: start with beer + wine only (Plan B), add spirits later.
Build-out surprises
Old GV buildings hide plumbing/electrical nightmares. Budget 20% overage and get a thorough inspection before signing.
Doing too much at once
Launch coffee first. Add bar in month 2. Refine retail over time. Don't try to perfect all three on day one.
Partnership tension
The operating agreement must be airtight. Define roles, decisions, exits, and money before you sign a lease.
